Sporting culture among Cuban populations on the island and in South Florida survived and suffered despite the turmoil, revolution, and corruption it faced. Pérez writes that "before the republic, there was baseball. After the revolution, there was baseball. And during all the years in between — there was baseball" (Pérez 1994). Another central spectacle that defines this people's sporting culture is jai alai. Journalist Dorothy Stanhope reports that "what the theater is to New York, jai alai is to Havana." Cuban diversity and the plurality of its identities provided space for a variety of national pastimes. These sporting activities actively engage with the island's Spanish colonial past and the post-colonial North American influence it continues to experience. The exportation and importation of players and culture reflect both the athletic aims of the country's various regimes and the commercial pressure from their North American neighbors. In other words, Spanish colonial influence manifested itself through the criollos, who introduced jai alai, bullfighting, and cockfighting. In contemporary neo-colonial terms, the North American sphere of influence led to the appropriation of baseball as the national sport.

I view this as a larger institutional matter of preservation and enclosure that occurs among Cuban communities on the island and their descendants in South Florida. To better understand and offer a more dynamic lens on Cuban-American sporting culture, this paper draws on Elinor Ostrom's socioeconomic analysis of the commons and, secondarily, applies John Fiske's anthropological work on fandom. Sport is a cultural manifestation that serves as a space for the social sciences and the humanities to engage in productive dialogue. In the book chapter "The Cultural Economy of Fandom," Fiske writes that fandom "is thus associated with the cultural tastes of subordinated formations of the people, particularly with those disempowered by any combination of gender, age, class and race." Since its inception, the Cuban diaspora has collectively suffered from subordination by different oppressors and importers of culture. The power relations imposed upon ethnicities and impoverished populations by different forms of colonialism lead to commodification, enclosure, and degradation of what I call Cuba's sporting culture commons. Those three threats are the exact terms that Ostrom uses in the volume *Understanding Knowledge as a Commons: From Theory to Practice* (2007). When introducing the concept of the knowledge commons, Hess and Ostrom propose "a new way of looking at knowledge as a shared resource, a complex ecosystem that is a commons — a resource shared by a group of people that is subject to social dilemmas" (Hess and Ostrom 2007). This framework can be applied to the sporting commons of Cuban history in the sense that culture simultaneously functions as knowledge. Fiske's theoretical methods provide auxiliary scaffolding for this interdisciplinary endeavor. Conceptualizing sporting sentiments as a commons adheres to Fiske's theory: "The cultural system works like the economic system to distribute its resources unequally and thus to distinguish between the privileged and the deprived" (31).

Spanish colonial castes and North American commercialization of the Cuban agricultural and hospitality sectors have persisted since the nineteenth century. The analysis of sporting culture allows us to equate the preservation of fanhood with the language Ostrom used to solve the tragedy of the commons — or, in this case, how athletic spectacles are propagated or compromised by the Cuban state and U.S. economic interests. According to Ostrom and Hess, "potential problems in the use, governance, and sustainability of a commons can be caused by some characteristic human behaviors that lead to social dilemmas such as competition for use, free riding, and overharvesting. Typical threats to knowledge commons are commodification or enclosure, pollution and degradation, and nonsustainability" (Hess and Ostrom 2007). Fanhood is another manifestation of knowledge. Given that the sporting cultural commons of Cuba faces these same threats from various institutions, we can combine these authors' frameworks. In line with the Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework, one must recognize the need for further research into the Cuban sporting commons, as the institutions in question operate in similar ways.

> The framework can be used to analyze static situations crafted by existing rules and relating to an unchanging physical world and relevant community. The framework can also be used to analyze dynamic situations where individuals develop new norms, new rules, and new physical technologies. Studying these developmental processes is more challenging than studies of fixed structures, but is very important for an understanding of the knowledge commons given the fast rate of change related to the physical world, the rules that are crafted to cope with new situations, and the enlarged community of producers and users. (Hess and Ostrom 41)

Across scholarship, concrete conclusions have been drawn that describe the island's cyclical sociopolitical changes, the ensuing restrictions (or, in Ostromian terms, enclosure), and a larger dialogue between individual identity and *cubanidad* (Cubanness). In *The Quality of Home Runs: The Passion, Politics, and Language of Cuban Baseball* (2008), Thomas F. Carter begins each chapter with a realistic account of how Cuban society practices baseball fandom. These aestheticized anecdotes provide insight into the spiritual meaning of sport. The introduction draws on the dilution of borders within *cubanidad*. Defining what is Cuban is a very difficult task due to the plurality of its people. To illustrate this complex problem of negotiating the meaning of being Cuban, Carter highlights Liván Hernández's North American success:

> Hernández's performance and isleños' (islanders') efforts at getting access to U.S. media broadcasts of the World Series to follow his exploits suggest an important aspect of the struggle over cubanidad. The island's shores neither contain nor demarcate the edges or reach of either Cuba or cubanidad. That both ideas are not tied to a specific location is suggested by the importance of Cuban athletes' endeavors, even (or perhaps especially) those who act without government officials' permission. At the same time, the production of the locality "Cuba" is vital to the struggles over what it means to be Cuban. Even then, the production of a locality's boundaries is not a cartographic certainty, but is dubious and ephemeral. (2)

Jai alai carries a similar structural complexity within its fanhood that complements baseball in the country's sporting commons. Although the sport is Basque in origin, *pelota vasca* is generalized and attributed as a wider Spanish import to Cuba's cultural commons. After independence from Spain in 1898, a United States temporary military government was established. Sporting culture and fanhood were already established on the island. However, when the national league's institutional support from North America ended, *cubanidad* permitted the enduring spirit of both sports. Morton explains that "by the end of Havana Jai Alai Fronton's ten-year contract, jai alai was more popular than bullfighting in Cuba. The city of Havana assumed control of the operation of the Jai Alai Fronton, known as The Palace of Screams (*El Palacio de los Gritos*)…" (70). The spectacle became internationally known; people came from Miami and South America to experience it. This explosion of the sport could be compared to that of a volcano — its lava (jai alai) "would forever burn itself into the minds and hearts of Cubans" (Morton 71).

Diversity and institutional pressures continuously threatened the expression of Cuban identity. Nevertheless, collective *cubanidad* used sports and fanhood to develop a national persona. For this reason, Elinor Ostrom's theoretical frameworks are useful for analyzing the Cuban sporting commons. In her principal work, she outlines eight design principles that describe how the commons can best survive in an environmental-economics sense. The Cuban sporting commons was treated as a self-governing natural resource. Her framework details the following design principles that solve the tragedy of the commons, which I adapt to my literary and cultural analysis:

- Clearly defined boundaries should be in place.
- Rules in use are well matched to local needs and conditions.
- Individuals affected by these rules can usually participate in modifying the rules.
- The right of community members to devise their own rules is respected by external authorities.
- A system for self-monitoring members' behavior has been established.
- A graduated system of sanctions is available.
- Community members have access to low-cost conflict-resolution mechanisms.
- Nested enterprises — that is, appropriation, provision, monitoring and sanctioning, conflict resolution, and other governance activities — are organized in a nested structure with multiple layers of activities. (Hess and Ostrom 2007)

Fiske enables this abstraction by concluding that "fans are among the most discriminating and selective of all formations of the people and the cultural capital they produce is the most highly developed and visible of all" (Fiske 48). This paper aims neither to apply economic terminology decoratively to sports studies, nor to suggest a Marxist or class-based discussion of Cuban sport. Observations of institutions drawn from non-fiction creative works show exactly how athletics transcends the violent pressures of politics and institutions. The movement and origins of the sports could suggest a non-Cuban narrative. However, recognition of the importation of both baseball and jai alai into expressions of Cuban identity forces a dialogue beneath the plurality of *cubanidad*. These two sports in particular demonstrate divergent trajectories of the Cuban sporting commons. Jai alai, which originated in the Basque Country, was played in Cuba but later declined sharply. The sport became dominated by gambling institutions and migrated its new-world cultural headquarters to the United States. Baseball, although it became popular due to American influence, evolved into something distinctly Cuban compared to the athletic practices established under Spanish rule. To argue how institutional pressures propagated baseball and compromised jai alai, this article draws on works such as *The Pride of Havana: A History of Cuban Baseball* by Roberto González Echevarría and *Basque Pelota: A Ritual, an Aesthetic* by Olatz González Abrisketa, alongside primary sources from periodicals and Wenceslao Gálvez.

This paper documents the diverging destinies of baseball and jai alai. Baseball thrived because it followed Ostrom's design principles: it was closely monitored by stakeholders and supported by the notion of *cubanidad*, and the state played a large role. The relationship between the revolution, Fidel Castro's regime, and the national physical-education body INDER demonstrates embedded cultural negotiation and monitoring of the Cuban sporting commons. Jai alai suffered because it succumbed to the common threats posed to the knowledge commons — enclosure, degradation, and commercialization, with betting and the lack of state support playing a massive role. Fiske provides the underlying scaffolding for understanding how fanhood operates and how both sports' cultural capital is seized or exploited. The neo-colonial model of the United States operated in complex ways, helping to promote and also to destroy jai alai, while repeated revolutionary measures led to the rejection of Spanish cultural manifestations. We must also reckon with race and immigration to understand the diaspora of *cubanidad* between *isleños* and those who migrated to Florida — and note that participation in jai alai remained almost uniquely Basque, so Cuban culture never developed the fanhood and athletic participation it would have needed to thrive.

## Ostrom in Operation

The commons is a general term referring to a resource shared by a group of people, with variable scale and boundaries (Hess and Ostrom 4). This article's analytical move is possible because "consideration of knowledge as a commons… suggests that the unifying thread in all commons resources is that they are jointly used, managed by groups of varying sizes and interests" (Hess and Ostrom 5). Sport as cultural knowledge works because these spectacles are collectively enjoyed and overseen by competing interests. The three threats that often compromise the commons can actively explain why baseball and jai alai met different fates. Ostrom writes that "typical threats to knowledge commons are commodification or enclosure, pollution and degradation, and nonsustainability" (Hess and Ostrom 4–5). Economic and cultural desires helped baseball and made jai alai unsustainable. In the discussion, collective action, self-governance, and social capital are the operational elements (Hess and Ostrom 6) that help explain both trajectories.

Ostrom implicitly justifies the deployment of her framework in a cultural-history paper, noting that it "can also be used to analyze dynamic situations where individuals develop new norms, new rules, and new physical technologies. Studying these developmental processes is more challenging than studies of fixed structures, but is very important for an understanding of the knowledge commons given the fast rate of change…" (Hess and Ostrom 41–42). Her theoretical grounding serves as analytical scaffolding, providing a universal set of intellectual building blocks (42). It is valid because these two trajectories experience distinct temporal pressures within the same commons.

Secondarily, sports anthropology explains how fandom operates in the commons. Fiske describes fandom as a "shadow cultural economy" that "lies outside that of the cultural industries yet shares features with them which more normal popular culture lacks" (Fiske 30). Ostrom guides the analysis while Fiske's framework complements it:

> Fan productivity is not limited to the production of new texts: it also participates in the construction of the original text and thus turns the commercial narrative or performance into popular culture. Fans are very participatory. Sports crowds wearing their teams' colors or rock audiences dressing and behaving like the bands become part of the performance. This melding of the team or performer and the fan into a productive community minimizes differences between artist and audience and turns the text into an event, not an art object. (Fiske 40)

The productive community within Cuba's sporting commons explains why baseball became the national sport and jai alai decayed. This article's move to call Cuban sporting culture a commons is directly supported by González Echevarría:

> Though an island, Cuba is really a frontier. In colonial times it was the first border between the Old and the New Worlds, later the embattled line between the Spanish Empire and the other European powers. Since the nineteenth century, and most poignantly in the past forty years, it has been either a bridge or a wall between itself and the United States. Crossings in either direction have always entailed a more or less radical transformation… its most visible and telling exhibition is found in popular culture and sports, particularly baseball and music. (González Echevarría 11)

The commons is exactly where the acceptance of baseball and the rejection of jai alai occur. Carter explains the acceptance of imported games as Cuban: "baseball, like any other game played around the world, is no more an Anglo sport in Latin America than Catholicism is an Italian religion. Institutional practices are modified, adapted, and incorporated into local practice and sensibilities" (Carter 13–14). He also identifies a broader Hispano-Caribbean pattern:

> As much as colonizers viewed sport as part of the civilizing processes associated with modernity, colonized peoples certainly did not passively accept these new games. Colonized peoples throughout the Caribbean demonstrated their own claims to national sovereignty and symbolically reversed power relationships, at least on the playing field… The expansion of Western sports into the societies of Africa, Asia, and Latin America at the cost of national and native games is a historical reflection of the institutional inequality resulting from economic dominance and neocolonialism. (15)

The result of outside pressures became the Cuban sporting commons when *cubanidad* claimed and appropriated these imported games. Pérez's "Between Baseball and Bullfighting: The Quest for Nationality in Cuba, 1868–1898" supports these conclusions: "a sport can serve as an instrument of empire and a means of social control, but in another place and at a different time, it can just as readily serve as a source of liberation and a means of nationality" (494). Ostrom's concept of a commons becomes a uniquely Cuban construction by using an imported custom as national raw material.

Other sports existed within the Cuban sporting commons, but baseball and jai alai are the most notable. Both were introduced to the island and have since become embedded in institutional life; both arrived in the nineteenth century and thrived in the first half of the 1900s, and both were affected by the revolution and the diaspora to the United States. *Cubanidad* is the factor that distinguishes their fates and enables their dialogue with Ostrom's framework. Other spectacles, such as bullfighting and cockfighting, also existed within Cuba's sporting commons; these uniquely Castilian customs were rejected at the end of the nineteenth century as *cubanidad* drew on anti-colonial sentiment, a contrast Pérez documents through the period's baseball press. The following sections turn to the two sports in question. Given that baseball became the national sport while jai alai was dismantled, there is more to be said about the former.

## The Baseball Trajectory

Baseball entered the commons through wealthy criollos educated in the United States but was rapidly transformed into a Cuban custom. The first revolutionary period evidences Ostrom's first design principle — clearly defined boundaries — as a community claims the game as its own. There were geographic and economic preconditions for baseball's national advancement. Pérez explains:

> As early as the 1830s and 1840s, and in numbers that increased all through the nineteenth century, Cubans traveled north… Thousands of young men and women, many representatives of the old landed elites and the expanding middle class, passed their formative years in the United States. (497)

This criollo channel stems from the entrepreneurial class becoming intertwined with American life during the sugar boom. González Echevarría affirms:

> As the sugar boom sparked by the demise of Haiti began, the Havana elite… pushed eastward and southeastward, toward Matanzas and Colón. They bought land, built mills, and began importing slaves at an unprecedented rate… Eventually, they began to send their sons to be educated in the United States, where they learned, among other things, the game of baseball. According to Gálvez's *El beisbol en Cuba*, "Mssrs. Tolón, Hernández, Amieva and Delgado, having received their education in the United States, organized the first baseball nine in Matanzas"… There was a substantial American presence not only in Havana but also in the Matanzas region… Matanzas' beautiful port and bay harbored many ships that sailed directly to New York or Boston. (81)

There is clear receptivity among Cubans to a North American import. Even though the sporting commons was influenced by external factors, the sport was quickly reconstituted through admiration for North American culture. Pérez agrees: "Cubans received baseball as a medium of North American culture, able to reproduce social relationships and reveal normative boundaries of North American society — a way to mediate the Cuban encounter with the north, to grow familiar with North American ways and participate in those ways" (499). Gálvez provides first-hand testimony to this negotiation of Ostrom's language of boundaries:

<blockquote lang="es"><p>Una de las ventajas principales que ha reportado el base-ball á nuestra juventud es, sin duda, el de alejarla del espectáculo nacional, ventaja que proclamo con orgullo de español, aunque algún quijote me tilde de antipatriota… que el base-ball es un espectáculo culto y los toros un espectáculo bárbaro. (15)</p></blockquote>

Pérez confirms the cultural boundary shift: "Cubans celebrated the modernity and progress implied in baseball, associated with the United States, and denounced the inhumanity and backwardness suggested by bullfighting, associated with Spain" (Pérez 505).

Through independence at the turn of the century, baseball's trajectory interacted with several of Ostrom's principles: rules matched local needs, communities could modify them, and the game was internally monitored by *cubanidad*. Gálvez describes baseball as an instrument of cultural regeneration that spanned social strata:

<blockquote lang="es"><p>El Base-Ball en Cuba, al reconocer generosamente que en su seno pueden ingresar indivíduos de todas las procedencias y condiciones, que tan solo se distingan por sus especiales aptitudes para la práctica y ejercicio del juego, ha salvado de la miseria, del abandono, de la ignorancia y quizás hasta del deshonor á muchos de esos que, si ántes fueron pilluelos alegres, hoy son jóvenes considerados en sociedad. (Gálvez 9)</p></blockquote>

In effect, Cuban communities define their own rules of entry based on merit, and on inclusion. The expansion of clubs across the island provides evidence of Ostrom's eighth design principle regarding nested enterprises. In 1878, after the Zanjón pact, "the number of amateur ball clubs in Havana increased rapidly all during the 1880s… Havana neighborhood teams (*clubes de barrio*) multiplied… Several Afro-Cuban societies organized baseball teams during the 1880s" (Pérez 501). This rapid growth reflects the organization of nested institutions within the emerging commons.

Ostrom's principle calling for external recognition of community boundaries manifested in the Spanish reaction to baseball. Pérez notes that "as early as 1873, soon after the organization of the Matanzas Baseball Club, government authorities banned baseball as an 'anti-Spanish activity'" (Pérez 511). Spanish colonial authorities perceived baseball as an organized counter-community — further support for the case of clearly defined boundaries.

During the republic and up until Castro's revolution, baseball's commercialization progressed; nevertheless, the commons prevailed through self-governance, conflict resolution, and graduated sanctions. González Echevarría frames the period: "in the first third of the twentieth century, Cuban baseball, particularly the professional kind, was deeply affected by the close political, economic, and cultural relations between the island and the United States… The first thirty years of the twentieth century were the Golden Age of the sport in Cuba" (112–113). Conflict resolution appeared during the 1946–47 crisis, with competing leagues, players' associations, and the Federación Nacional working to preserve the commons; "whereupon Suárez and Maduro, aided by wizard promoter Emilio de Armas, formed a corporation and built the new stadium in a year" (23). The unionization of professional players also merits attention: "Tomás de la Cruz and Napoleón Reyes took the initiative, formally and legally creating the Asociación Nacional de Peloteros Profesionales de Cuba… the owners were not fooled and agreed immediately to a series of demands on salaries and working conditions" (24). Here principles six and seven are at work — graduated sanctioning of the sport's commercial capture, and low-cost conflict resolution. González Echevarría's reading of the Habana/Almendares rivalry illustrates the commons at work: "Afro-Cuban syncretic faith and liturgy, like music, had slowly been bonding Cuban society in a movement that went from the bottom up, from the descendants of former slaves to the descendants of former masters" (41). Baseball does the cultural work that formal institutions cannot capture: "by virtue of being a collective activity baseball is also, like all sports, a ritual that retains an aura of sacredness… this sacredness is sublimated or deflected into nationalism" (12–13).

INDER (the National Institute of Sports, Physical Education and Recreation) reconstituted the conditions of baseball beneath the greater commons. After 1959, the Castro regime replaced commercial institutions with state-sponsored ones; baseball's popular roots persisted and its self-governing qualities reshaped themselves, while jai alai was compromised as capitalist interests left the island. The regime's intervention is clear: "the Castro regime has succeeded in dramatically changing culture in at least one regard. After much effort, the government was able to build on Cubans' existing interest in sports — already substantial before the Revolution — so as to develop a national revolutionary sports consciousness" (Bunck 112). There is a stark difference between *building on* and *replacing*. Baseball had already rooted itself within the cultural commons, so it did not matter whether the state or the market interacted with it; *cubanidad* retained domain. Bunck explains INDER's design: "INDER would oversee sports education in every school and institution on the island… In 1967, the government abolished all professional sports and announced that admission to all sports events would be free" (117) — new rules well matched to local needs, the second Ostrom principle. Huish and Darnell add that "INDER's new model strove to deconstruct such class division by creating public training centers and ensuring public opportunities for participation regardless of economic class" (148). Still, the commons faced the threat of commercialization Ostrom identifies as typical; as González Echevarría observes, "the current Cuban regime has really profited from the strength of Cuban sports before 1959" (8), which validates the framework because popular roots predated the state's attempt to capture the commons. Carter deepens the argument with his *aplauso de ponche* passage: fans "know that they had an effect on the game" (111), and "instead of treating Cuban baseball fans as socially deviant or marginal members of society, I argue that they embody a central cross section of Cuban society cutting across class, racial, and political lines" (112). Collectively, the love of baseball across social strata secured its place within the commons.

*Granma*, the revolution's continually operating periodical, published an article this year describing the ongoing relationship with the United States and offering proof of a victorious commons beneath *cubanidad*. In response to U.S. visa denials before the 2026 World Baseball Classic, the official statement declares that "the U.S. authorities know that baseball is an intrinsic part of the history and pride of the Cuban people," and that "we will return… with our Cultural Heritage, which is baseball" (INDER). The article also evokes the longstanding relationship the nation-state holds with the sport, folding the trajectory's continuity into official self-understanding. The current conditions of baseball legitimize Ostrom's framework: baseball is a Cuban cultural heritage that transcends mere sport, and institutional pressures are defeated by whatever political configuration threatens the commons.

## The Emergence and Dismantling of Jai Alai

Jai alai entered the sporting commons as a peninsular import whose participation remained largely Basque. The popular roots that continue to sustain baseball are nonexistent here; from the outset, the boundary conditions for an Ostrom commons explanation were weak.

Fundamentally, jai alai maintains a uniquely Basque cultural connotation. González Abrisketa frames the game: "more than simply the site of a game, the fronton is the place of celebration par excellence; it is generally the main square of the village, and serves as the communal hearth for community events" (*Basque Pelota* 16). Furthermore, "the centrality of pelota in public space demands of it an interpretation that transcends the mere sportive. The game's liturgical form… clearly make it a foundation ritual and a fundamental representation of community" (16). Jai alai maintains itself almost exclusively within a Basque cultural commons; it never took shape in the Cuban one.

When jai alai arrived in the new world, it carried commercial enclosure: "by the end of the nineteenth century, pelota outside of Euskal Herria was no longer a mere recreational outlet… some people emigrated specifically to play pelota, contracted by companies in the target country. Most were Basque… The pelotaris, now professionals, crossed borders with their instruments on their shoulders" (64). The structure of professional jai alai is contractual and migratory from inception — the opposite of baseball's popular trajectory within *cubanidad*. Even the name derives from a marketing approach. Morton explains that "enterprisers who promoted the sport in Cuba recognized the global value of a marketable brand name… the Cuban promoters, so it is said, adopted the name of jai alai, 'happy festival' in Basque" (62). The naming itself describes commercial enclosure, and betting-based motivations further prove Ostrom's threats: even in its most casual form, with "Basque seamen on shore leave [who] threw the ball… hoping to win a few extra coins" (Morton 61), jai alai in Cuba was uniquely Basque and closely associated with gambling, outside *cubanidad*.

Jai alai was a commercial institution from its arrival: Mazzantini's lease deal with the American occupation, General Wood's facilitation, and a constant dependence on gambling infrastructure. Dorothy Stanhope's 1904 *New York Times* article gives a first-hand account of these prerequisites to jai alai's fate: "what the theatre is to New York Jai Alai is to Havana… Two evenings a week and Sunday afternoon the Fronton is crowded" (Stanhope). The keyword is *theatre*: participation and fanhood were performative and commercial, not active preservation. She affirms that "all tourists are directed to it as one of the show places of the city," that "the Fronton is owned by a stock company composed of Cubans and Spaniards," and that "one can imagine going to a horse race without placing money on a favorite or to a baseball game without making wagers, but at Jai Alai, betting is the order of the day" (Stanhope). Corruption within the betting demonstrates degradation: rumors that "the games are sold" circulated continuously (Stanhope). The promotion of the spectacle confirms the enclosure — Mazzantini proposed to "pay rent to the city and construct at his own expense an architect-designed sports arena," noting that "the city had to supply extra police to keep order when the fans got out of control" (Morton 66). *Cubanidad* could not include jai alai within its commons because fans were positioned as disorderly customers rather than participants in cultural-capital development. General Wood, "the jai alai fan, used his political authority to pave the way for a state-of-the-art commercial arena" (Morton 65) — the inverse of baseball's local clubs sprouting across classes and races. Violence also polluted the game: Morton documents that "at least one well-known Havana gentleman committed suicide because he lost the family hacienda to his bookmaker," and that "in the stands bloody fistfights broke out between rival fans" (70). Financial interests superseded the development of true cultural capital.

The same revolution that reconstituted the conditions for baseball neglected the continuation of jai alai. "But every party has to come to an end," Morton writes; "the revolutionary Fidel Castro arrived, and jai alai in Cuba died" (72). Jai alai represented betting, foreign labor, and elite clientele, all ideologically opposed to INDER's model. González Abrisketa records the decline: "the communist system considered betting a capitalist activity; and frontons shut down in all the countries where leftist absolutist regimes were established. The Palacio de los Gritos in Havana… met the same fate three years after the Cuban Revolution of 1959" (67). Morton is blunt: "there was no jai alai in life after the Cuban Revolution" (73). Had jai alai held the same deep roots within *cubanidad* as baseball, there would have been counter-mobilization, popular protest, or neighborhood clubs rising in its defense. The lack of self-governance in the face of commercial capture explains its eradication.

After the revolution, jai alai was displaced from Cuba to Florida, but even with promotion from the Cuban diaspora it could not sustain itself outside its Basque labor base. González Abrisketa notes that the 1959 revolution "helped to foster the spread of jai-alai in Florida," since the roughly 200,000 Cubans who emigrated "were quite prepared to visit American frontons in order to indulge their passion for gambling, which was banned by the Castro regime" (184) — proof that jai alai was more a habit than a true communal fan culture. The 1988 strike illustrates the institutional fracture: fronton owners "refused to recognize the IJAPA, and began to threaten and use unfair labor practices against the players who joined it, which provoked a strike that lasted three years" (180) — a failure of the low-cost conflict resolution essential to a functioning commons. NBC News's coverage of the Miami fronton's closure documents the final, Ostromian non-sustainability: "while in the 1980s, the game attracted thousands of spectators, in recent years stadiums were filled with only a couple dozen attendees… the sport faced even tighter competition in Florida with the arrival of professional baseball, basketball and hockey" (Fernandez and Bellamy-Walker). Jai alai survives in the Cuban diaspora only through capital investment, not demand stemming from *cubanidad*.

## Conclusion

Ostrom's framework allowed this investigation to explain two diverging paths within the same cultural commons. The same Cuban socioeconomic ecosystem housed both sports; the same colonial and neocolonial pressures attacked both; the same revolutionary state produced distinct outcomes within the greater notion of *cubanidad*. Identifying the Cuban sporting commons adds a new lens for sports studies: it shows that the island's diversity and social strata preferred one sport over the other. Solving the tragedy of the commons depends on participants, knowledge, and culture. Baseball evaded and demanded certain conditions from the state and the free market, whereas jai alai was compromised from its importation. This paper proves the validity of Ostrom's methodology because it shows the victorious qualities of collective action, self-governance, and social capital operating institutionally through "rules, decisions, and behaviors people make in groups in relation to their shared resource" (Hess and Ostrom 10) to survive outside pressures.
