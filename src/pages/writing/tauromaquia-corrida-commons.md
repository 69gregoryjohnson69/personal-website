The Spanish corrida is most often debated as a moral question — cruelty versus tradition. This framing is too narrow and obscures what is actually at stake. Both progressives and conservatives treat the bullfight as a fixed object to be either abolished or preserved, ignoring the fact that the corrida is a centuries-old institution whose rules, participants, and meanings have always been negotiated by the social actors who use it. This paper argues that the corrida functions as a self-governing cultural commons in the sense developed by Charlotte Hess and Elinor Ostrom, and that the Spanish state's recent legislative interventions — Ley 18/2013, Ley 10/2015, and the Constitutional Tribunal's STC 177/2016 ruling — together constitute an act of enclosure that displaces the multi-stakeholder governance through which the corrida has historically reproduced itself. Reframing the contemporary debate in these terms shifts the question from whether the corrida should exist to who has the right to decide how, where, and under what conditions it continues.

## I. The Knowledge Commons and Its Enclosure

Hess and Ostrom distinguish a "common-pool resource" from "common property." A common-pool resource is a shared resource system; common property is one particular legal arrangement of rights to such a system. As they put it, "Shared resource systems, called common-pool resources, are types of economic goods, independent of particular property rights" (4–5). This distinction matters for the corrida because no single entity owns bullfighting in any property-rights sense, yet bullfighting is unmistakably a shared resource, produced and reproduced by interconnected actors. Hess and Ostrom further argue that any commons analysis must address three central questions: equity in appropriation and contribution, efficiency in management, and sustainability over the long term (6).

From decades of empirical work on long-enduring commons institutions, Ostrom identified eight design principles characteristic of communities that successfully self-govern shared resources without state imposition or market privatization. Hess and Ostrom summarize them as follows: (1) clearly defined boundaries; (2) rules in use well matched to local needs and conditions; (3) individuals affected by the rules can participate in modifying them; (4) the right of community members to devise their own rules is respected by external authorities; (5) a system for self-monitoring members' behavior; (6) a graduated system of sanctions; (7) low-cost conflict-resolution mechanisms accessible to community members; and (8) governance activities organized in a nested structure with multiple layers (6–7). The presence of these principles is what allows a commons to remain robust across generations. Their disruption is what causes a commons to collapse or to be captured by external actors.

Hess and Ostrom describe enclosure as the principal modern threat to a knowledge commons. Enclosure is not a single mechanism but a tendency: "the ability of new technologies to 'capture' resources that were previously unowned, unmanaged, and thus, unprotected" (12). In the contemporary context, capture need not involve physical privatization. Legal designation, intellectual property regimes, and centralized cultural policy can each enclose intangible resources by transferring rule-making authority away from the communities that had previously sustained them.

One dimension of this transfer of authority is captured by John Fiske's theory of fan cultural economy. Fiske distinguishes between popular cultural capital, which is "shadow" capital produced and accumulated by audiences and participants outside formal institutions, and official cultural capital, which is supported by schools, museums, and the state (30–31). When the state designates a popular practice as official heritage, it does not merely add legal protection: it converts popular cultural capital into official capital, taking with it the authority to define what the practice means. This is the key Fiskean move that Section III identifies in the 2013 designation.

## II. The Corrida as a Common-Pool Resource

Adrian Shubert's economic history of the Spanish bullfight describes a multi-stakeholder institution from its modern beginnings. "Before and beyond all else, the bullfight was a business," Shubert writes; its purpose "was to make money: for private institutions and public purposes as well as for the growing myriad of individuals for whom it was a source of income, sometimes the sole source of income" (17). He characterizes it as a "cultural industry" that brought together "bullfighters, breeders, promoters, critics, and fans" with divergent and often conflictual interests (17). Shubert also documents the deep institutional embeddedness of the bullfight in religious and welfare organizations: the Royal Maestranzas of Seville and Ronda were granted local monopolies on bullfights in the eighteenth century, while hospitals in Madrid and Valencia were given the proceeds of bullfights to fund their care of the sick and the poor (18). The corrida did not begin as a folk practice that was later commercialized; it has always operated through layered institutions with competing claims on the same shared resource.

This complexity is precisely what makes Ostrom's framework useful. The corrida's resource system includes the plaza, the ganadería, the labor of toreros and cuadrillas, the regulatory and ritual rules of the lidia, and the cultural meaning produced by successful or unsuccessful corridas. Its users and providers include matadors, breeders, promoters, critics, the público, ecclesiastical and welfare institutions, municipal authorities, and regional governments. Rather than survey the full history of this institution, the rest of this section applies five of Ostrom's eight design principles to demonstrate how the corrida has historically satisfied the conditions of a robust commons.

The first principle — clearly defined boundaries — operates in tauromaquia at several levels. The lidia bull is defined as a distinct breed: raised on dedicated ganaderías, traceable by lineage and brand, and certified for the ring only when its physical and temperamental qualities meet established standards. The ring, the cuadrilla, and the categories of performers (matador, picador, banderillero) are each bounded by regulation and tradition. The boundary most relevant for the cultural commons, however, is the one drawn between aficionados and casual spectators. As Fiske notes, fans "discriminate fiercely" (34); bullfighting fans use a sharp distinction between the aficionado who understands the lidia and the entendido or simple spectator who does not. Hemingway dramatizes this in *The Sun Also Rises*, where the hotelkeeper Montoya recognizes Jake Barnes as a true aficionado:

> He always smiled as though bull-fighting were a very special secret between the two of us; a rather shocking but really very deep secret that we knew about. . . . It would not do to expose it to people who would not understand. (150)

The remark is not merely social. It functions as a boundary-marking practice that distinguishes those qualified to evaluate the corrida from those who are not, and therefore distinguishes those whose judgments contribute to the commons' rules from those whose judgments do not.

The second principle — proportional equivalence between costs and benefits — is visible in the distribution of risk and reward across stakeholders. The matador bears the risk of injury and death. The ganadero bears capital risk on his herd over multi-year breeding cycles. The cuadrilla and plaza workers bear the value of their labor. The público bears the costs of attendance and the emotional risk of witnessing a poor or fatal performance. The benefits are similarly distributed: economic for breeders and promoters, professional reputation for matadors and critics, and cultural meaning for the público. The legitimacy of the corrida depends on these benefits remaining roughly proportional to the costs each stakeholder bears. When the matador is gored, the system depends on aficionados, critics, and other matadors recognizing the meaning of that cost. Hemingway makes the alternative visible through the waiter in *The Sun Also Rises*: "What are bulls? Animals. Brute animals," the waiter dismisses, calling the matador's injury "all for fun" (216–17). The waiter is not wrong about the animal cost; he is positioned outside the community that treats that cost as the price of something else — the artistic, ethical, and existential value that the aficionado community recognizes as the proper return on the matador's risk. Without that recognition, the cost is gratuitous; within the community, it is proportional to the cultural capital the corrida produces. The community itself, in Ostrom's terms, is what makes the proportionality real.

The third principle — that those affected by the rules can participate in modifying them — is met through the multiple negotiating forums that have historically generated and revised the corrida's rules. Reglamentos taurinos at the regional level codify the formal rules of the lidia, but their content reflects the evolving consensus of breeders' associations, presidentes de plaza, matadors' professional bodies, and the público whose reactions in the ring effectively sanction or reward each performance. Critics and the taurine press constitute an additional negotiating forum; their reviews and ongoing debates affect breeders' reputations, promoters' choices, and matadors' careers. No single actor has unilateral authority over the rules. They are revised through interaction among the stakeholders Shubert names.

The fifth and sixth principles — self-monitoring and graduated sanctions — are exercised in real time by the público, which functions as the corrida's primary monitoring body. The crowd's reactions (silence, ovation, white handkerchief, pañolada, whistles) constitute a graduated sanctioning system that conveys real consequences to matadors, ganaderos, and presidentes alike. A bull rejected as defective before the fight, a matador whistled out of the ring, a ganadería whose stock is consistently judged poor: each is a sanction applied not by external authority but by the community of users itself. Critics in the taurine press extend and formalize these sanctions in writing, and the cumulative reputation of any matador, breeder, or plaza is the long-run aggregate of such judgments. The system is not perfect, but it is recognizably the kind of internal monitoring that Ostrom's framework identifies as essential to commons sustainability.

The eighth principle — nested enterprises — is the most consequential for the contemporary debate. The corrida has always operated through nested institutions: the ganadería at one level, the plaza at another, the municipal authority above that, and the regional government above that, with cofradías, religious orders, the Maestranzas, and welfare hospitals occupying their own layers in earlier centuries. Shubert documents this nested structure when he describes the Sevillian and Ronda Maestranzas as "privileged corporations of aristocrats" granted local monopolies on bullfights, and the hospitals of Madrid and Valencia receiving bullfight revenue as a "comfortable alternative to taxation" (17–18). Each layer governs the aspects of the practice relevant to its level. The corrida in Pamplona is not the corrida in Sevilla is not the corrida in Madrid; each draws on shared norms while maintaining local rules, local meanings, and local stakeholders. The variation across regions is not a defect to be smoothed over but a structural feature of the commons.

Taken together, these principles describe an institution that has, for centuries, governed itself in the manner Ostrom identifies as characteristic of long-enduring commons. The threat to this self-governance does not come from animal-rights advocacy or from market commercialization, both of which the system has long absorbed. It comes from a more recent and more comprehensive form of state capture.

## III. The 2013 Designation as Enclosure

In 2013, the Spanish government passed Ley 18/2013, which formally declared tauromaquia "patrimonio cultural" of Spain. This was followed in 2015 by Ley 10/2015, the law for the safeguarding of Intangible Cultural Heritage. In Hess and Ostrom's terms, these laws are not neutral protections of a popular practice. They are an enclosure of a previously self-governing commons.

Gema Carrera Díaz has provided the sharpest critique of this move. She argues that the 2015 law instrumentalizes the language of intangible heritage — "diversidad cultural," "identidad," "comunidad," "participación social" — in order to legitimize the consolidation of cultural traditions that serve a centralizing "nacional-Estatal" identity rather than reflecting genuine cultural diversity (10–11). Tauromaquia is, for her, the central case. The designation, she argues, transforms intangible heritage into

<blockquote lang="es"><p>una forma de instrumentalizar políticamente las identidades culturales, homogeneizándolas, desvitalizándolas y mercantilizándolas, utilizando el "patrimonio inmaterial" como coartada. (13)</p></blockquote>

Three of Carrera Díaz's specific charges map directly onto Ostrom's design principles. First, the law operates by *deslocalización* — removing the practice from its local and regional contexts and abstracting it to the national level — which violates the nested-enterprises principle. Second, it engages in *esencialización* — codifying a single, españolizado version of the practice — which violates the principle that rules in use should be well matched to local needs and conditions. Third, and most directly, the law transfers competencies from the autonomous communities and from the participating "comunidades, grupos e individuos" to the central state acting through parliamentary groups, "motu proprio y sin responder a reclamaciones de colectivos sociales" (13). This violates the third and fourth principles: that those affected by the rules participate in modifying them, and that external authorities respect community rule-making.

The Constitutional Tribunal's ruling in STC 177/2016 makes this enclosure legally explicit. Catalunya's Ley 28/2010 had prohibited bullfighting within the autonomous community, an action grounded in the Generalitat's competencies over public spectacles and animal welfare. Fifty senators of the Partido Popular challenged the law, arguing that bullfighting was the cultural patrimony of all Spaniards and therefore fell under state jurisdiction per Article 149.1.28 of the Constitution. The Tribunal ruled 8–3 in favor of the PP (Mulà 410–11). As Anna Mulà summarizes the holding, the Tribunal determined that

<blockquote lang="es"><p>el alcance de la competencia exclusiva de la Generalidad sobre espectáculos públicos sólo permite dictar normas reguladoras, pero no prohibitivas, cuando se trata de espectáculos taurinos por ser patrimonio cultural de todos los españoles. (411)</p></blockquote>

Autonomous communities may regulate the corrida, including measures protecting animals; they may not prohibit it (Mulà 436). Mulà also notes the dissenting opinion of Magistrates Asua Batarrita and Valdés Dal-ré, who observed that state protections of cultural patrimony have historically applied to movable and immovable property against exportation and despoliation, not to intangible practices like tauromaquia (412).

Read through Ostrom's framework, STC 177/2016 directly removes collective-choice authority from one level of the nested governance structure. Catalunya, as a unit within the nested system, had exercised the right of its constituent community to modify the rules under which the corrida did or did not operate within its territory. The Tribunal's ruling tells Catalunya — and by extension every other autonomous community — that this right is foreclosed. The state has captured a level of rule-making that previously belonged to the regional layer of the commons. The fourth design principle is violated explicitly: the right of community members to devise their own rules is no longer respected by external authorities, because the external authority has redefined the rule itself as state property.

A second axis of enclosure deserves attention because it captures the practice from a different direction. Pro-corrida political actors, particularly on the conservative side, have linked the continuation of bullfighting to the preservation of the Spanish dehesa — the agrosilvopastoral landscape of Iberia in which fighting bulls are raised. The dehesa is a low-intensity land-use system in which "plantas leñosas perennes y cultivos herbáceos" coexist with grazing animals, classified by the European Union as a high natural value system and located mostly in disadvantaged peninsular zones with rural depopulation challenges (Sanes et al. 177). Sanes et al. argue that "la existencia de las ganaderías de lidia para la cría del toro bravo está íntimamente ligada al mantenimiento y conservación del ecosistema de alta biodiversidad de la dehesa española, con una carga animal baja" (188–89). The dehesa is real, its ecological value is real, and the link between fighting-bull ranching and dehesa maintenance is real. But the deployment of these facts in political discourse converts the corrida into a single essentialist object — Spain's most representative cultural and ecological landscape — that must be preserved as a national whole. This is the same essentialist move Carrera Díaz identifies in the legislative discourse: a multifaceted, multi-stakeholder practice is collapsed into a national symbol that can then be defended or attacked as a unit. The conservative populist defense and the abolitionist attack turn out to be mirror images. Both treat the corrida as one thing, owned by Spain, rather than as a nested commons whose governance properly belongs to the layers of stakeholders who actually sustain it.

## IV. Implications for Self-Governance

What follows from this analysis is neither the conclusion that the corrida must continue nor that it must end. It is the conclusion that the question itself is wrongly posed when the state poses it. The implication of Ostrom's framework is that the conditions for the corrida's continuation or termination should be determined by the layered stakeholders who use and provide the resource, not by a centralizing legal designation imposed from above. Three concrete consequences follow.

First, the constitutional foreclosure of regional prohibition should be recognized as a violation of the commons' nested structure. A Catalunya that no longer wishes to host bullfights is exercising precisely the collective-choice right that a long-enduring commons depends on. A robust commons can shrink as well as grow; what it cannot survive is the loss of local rule-making to a central authority. STC 177/2016, by treating the corrida as state property whose continuation is non-negotiable at the regional level, breaks the very institution it claims to protect. Recognizing this means accepting that some regions may, in time, decide to discontinue bullfighting — and that this is a legitimate outcome of self-governance, not a betrayal of cultural heritage.

Second, the conservative deployment of cultural patrimony and dehesa preservation is not a defense of the commons but a different mode of capture. When the practice is reduced to a national symbol whose continuation is the test of Spanish identity, the público is no longer a participant in a self-governing community; it is recruited as a constituency for a political program. The Fiskean conversion of popular cultural capital into official cultural capital is what the designation accomplishes, and it does so on behalf of one political tendency. Defending the corrida by tying it to the marca España does not protect it from enclosure — it is the enclosure.

Third, the scholarly task is to identify and protect the layers of governance that have allowed the practice to evolve. The plurality of stakeholders Shubert documents — matadors, breeders, promoters, critics, fans, ecclesiastical institutions, welfare organizations, municipal authorities — and the plurality of layers Ostrom's framework names — local, regional, national, supranational — together constitute the actual governance system of the corrida. The 2013 patrimonialization, its 2015 elaboration, and its constitutional defense in 2016 have replaced this plural system with a single national logic. The corrida that emerges from this enclosure may continue to look like a corrida, but it will no longer be self-governing in the sense that has sustained it for centuries.

## Conclusion

The corrida is neither a moral monolith to be defended nor an indefensible practice to be abolished. It is a centuries-old multi-stakeholder commons that has reproduced itself through the polycentric, nested governance Ostrom and Hess identify as the hallmark of long-enduring institutions. The 2013 and 2015 heritage designations, together with the 2016 Constitutional ruling against Catalunya, constitute an enclosure of this commons — a transfer of rule-making authority from the layered stakeholder community to the central state. The conservative defense of the practice, far from protecting its self-governance, has accelerated its capture by collapsing it into a single national symbol.

Returning the corrida to its commons logic does not predetermine its future. It would mean restoring the regional and local collective-choice authority that the Constitutional Tribunal removed. It would mean recognizing that the layers of stakeholders Shubert names — and not the state — are the parties whose decisions should govern the practice. And it would mean allowing the natural cycle of afición, criticism, breeding, promotion, regulation, and local participation to determine where, how, and whether the corrida continues to exist, without that question being foreclosed in either direction by a law passed in Madrid.
